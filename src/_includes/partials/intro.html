About 3 – 4000 years ago, this 'upper torso of a dancing woman' was painted.

About 512 years ago, Leonardo Di Vinci painted the Mona Lisa.

And 181 years ago, the world's first selfie was taken.

It took about another 100 years from that selfie moment for cameras and their output to be accessible to the masses. Before that, portraiture painting was one of the few mediums available for recording and preserving people (and their character). Its output scale of one, the skill needed (to do a good job), and cost of materials made painting portraits scarce, expensive, and available to just a few.

I'm writing this at the end of year 2020 CE. Given you have access to this article* (*and therefore in access of an internet-connected device, with most importantly, a screen (and perhaps a camera), you probably know all of the the above innately.

You also understand the contextual differences between these two portraits. One is of someone important; laboriously painted by a master, and probably kept somewhere safe without flash photography or sunlight. The other is a spontaneous, hastily-shot photo* from a cheap (perhaps disposable) camera; printed at Kmart, and dogeared from years of wallet-living. (*you can decide who is the important one...)

If you're like me however, you may not have considered that the 'switch' from portraiture to photography (as the main method to record and preserve memories of people) is actually an ever-evolving relationship. In the age of FaceTune and on the cusp of Deepfake, photos can no longer be assumed to be an objective record. Nor could they ever be. As John Berger put it:
